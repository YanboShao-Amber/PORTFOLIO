Overview

Game Type: Farming & Life Sim · Cozy

Quiet Town is a Stardew-Valley-style farming sim: plant, water and harvest crops across a day/night cycle, fish in a timing mini-game, place machines, trade with travelling merchants, and raise your standing with the townsfolk. It began from a Godot course foundation — then I took it over solo and did the real design work on top: a fully documented, iterated economy-balance model, a rarity-driven fish-variety system, and a playtest-and-telemetry loop that tunes the numbers from data rather than feel. This page is a designer's tour of what I consider the project's real highlights.

Quiet Town runs on the classic cozy-sim loop, measured out one in-game day at a time. Each day you earn, spend and invest — and every number on this page is tuned around that single unit. Here is the moment-to-moment flow a player actually lives through, from soil to town square.

THE DAY · 一天

Day / night & weather

The world turns on a day/night cycle with weather — rain waters your crops for free. The in-game day is the heartbeat of the whole design: it's the pacing unit the entire five-day economy model is balanced against.

FARM · 农事

Plant → water → harvest → sell

Four crops — wheat, corn, tomato, pumpkin — each with its own growth time and payout. Fast wheat pays little and often; slow pumpkin is a long, high-value bet. Choosing what to plant is the player's first real economic decision every day.

FISH · 垂钓

A timing mini-game

Fishing is an active skill check, not a passive wait: land the timing and you reel in a fish. It's the player's early cash flow before crops mature — and, as the Fish System tab shows, it's built to stay more rewarding than any automated substitute.

BUILD · 建造

Blueprints & machines

Sprinklers, fishers and scarecrows are placed from unlocked blueprints onto the grid. Every placement spends materials — so automation is never free, and the town you build is a running record of the choices you paid for.

TOWN · 小镇

Trade, townsfolk & comfortQoL

Travelling merchants (Courier, Cat, Mouse) buy and sell, and townsfolk like Mira run their own relationship, gift and quest systems. Wrapped around all of it is deliberate quality-of-life work: hint buttons, a player-pointer highlighter, and full gamepad support tested alongside mouse and keyboard.

I rebuilt the economy from a model, not from feel.

The course foundation shipped with 50-item debug stacks and machines you could place for free forever — fine for a tutorial, fatal for a real economy. I wrote a running design contract (Day 1 raw baseline → Day 2 v1/v2 → Day 4 v3) that fixed the starting state, priced every action, and proved the five-day curve on paper before a line of balance code changed. This tab is the heart of the highlight.

Course baseline

No scarcity, no choice

Every item started at 50, coins at 250. Machines cost a one-time material fee to unlock, then placed for free forever. Nothing the player did carried a lasting cost.

Balanced v2 / v3

Every action has a price

Start cut to 150 coins and a real 27-crop opening (exactly enough to plant 16 tiles). Blueprints now cost coins to unlock; each machine placement then spends a material recipe — so automation is an ongoing investment.

Four crops, four risk profiles

Reward rises with the wait

Coin rewards are set so the long-run return per tile per day strictly increases with growth time. Fast wheat is safe pocket money; pumpkin is a twelve-day commitment that pays the most. The order is a deliberate, protected invariant.

Blueprints & placement recipes

Unlock once, pay to place

Blueprint coin costs are paced to be first affordable on their target day (Day 3 / 4 / 5). Then every placement re-spends materials — a second machine costs again, keeping automation tied to continued farming and fishing.

Placement is atomic: materials are deducted only after tile legality and setup succeed — a failed placement costs nothing.

The result: choices with teeth

Across the modelled five days, no single repeatable income source exceeds 50% (fishing 47.7%, farming 41.4%, the one-off quest 10.9%) — so neither activity trivialises the other. And spending competes: buying a Cowboy hat drains the very Wood the Fisher needs, creating real opportunity cost instead of a checklist. Balance came out of the spreadsheet, then got confirmed in playtests.

The course shipped fishing as one generic FISH item — you caught it, you sold it, done. I turned it into a rarity-driven species system that gives fishing texture without inflating the economy, and threaded it through inventory, trade, recipes and telemetry with a save-safe, append-only migration.

"Any Fish" recipes that protect the rares

The Fisher's build cost is Any Fish × 3 — a category, not a specific species. When it charges, it consumes fish in a fixed order: lowest sell price first. So placing a machine eats your Gray Carp and leaves the Golden Koi and Red Snapper untouched. The player never has to babysit which fish gets spent, and the trophy catch stays a trophy — one clean rule doing quiet, player-friendly work.

The part I'm proudest of isn't a feature — it's the process. Every balance change was proposed on paper, measured against a written playtest protocol, logged as telemetry, and rolled out in small reversible steps. This is how I'd want to work on a real team. Each card opens for detail.

Playtest protocols Telemetry JSON Staged migration Rollback plans Data-driven tuning
01 Core
Written playtest protocols
In one line

Each build ships with a checklist that says exactly what to test and what "pass" means.

Details ▾
  • The fish-species protocol asks the tester to log 20+ manual catches, run 5+ automatic Fisher cycles, and buy/sell every species — with expected values written next to each.
  • Placement tests explicitly check the rare-protection order: given mixed fish, the Fisher must consume Gray/Silver and leave Koi/Snapper unchanged.
  • Every flow is repeated across mouse, keyboard and Xbox controller; cancel paths (right-click / Esc / B) must place nothing and cost nothing.

A protocol turns "it feels okay" into a checklist someone else could run. That's the difference between testing and hoping.

02 Data
Telemetry, not vibes
In one line

The game logs structured JSON events so balance can be read off numbers.

Details ▾
  • Catch events tag their source (manual vs automatic_fisher), species, and inventory after — so drop-rate reality can be checked against the 50/30/15/5 design.
  • Machine placement logs category_materials_consumed, recording exactly which fish were spent — proof the rare-protection order actually fired.
  • Fishing attempts, successes, failures and durations are all counted, feeding the success-rate and per-day income assumptions in the economy model.

Telemetry closes the loop: the model predicts, the log measures, and the gap tells me what to tune next.

03 Architecture
Staged, reversible migration
In one line

The fish system shipped in seven small phases, each with its own rollback.

Details ▾
  • It began as a read-only architecture audit — mapping every place the old generic FISH was referenced before touching any of them.
  • New item enums are append-only (added after existing values, never reordered) so old saves and logs never break.
  • Data first, then manual fishing, then the automatic Fisher, then recipes, then UI cleanup — each phase with runtime tests and a stated rollback risk.

Small reversible steps mean a broken phase is contained, not catastrophic — the way you change live systems safely.

04 Balance
A stated tuning priority
In one line

When data disagrees with the model, there's a fixed order for what to change.

Details ▾
  • Tune in order: (1) manual fishing reward, (2) blueprint coin cost, (3) starting coins, (4) crop coin rewards.
  • Crop growth times are off-limits — they define the four crops' identities, so they're the last thing I'd touch, not the first.
  • The five-day model is re-validated after each change against a checklist: does any repeatable source exceed 50%, do purchases still land on their target days.

Knowing what not to touch is as much a design skill as knowing what to change.

The starting point was a Godot course project; the design work on top — the economy model, the fish-variety system, the playtest-and-telemetry loop, and the QoL polish — is mine. Art and audio use the Ninja Adventure, Tiny Swords and Sprout Lands asset packs.
